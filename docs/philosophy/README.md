# The Philosophy of Olive Branch Network

This document is a compilation, not new authorship — it records the reasoning
that produced the Charter, so a future reader can see *why* the Charter says
what it says, without needing to re-derive it.

## Why does a system like OBN need to exist?

Most public-benefit systems begin from obligation: taxation, tithing mandates,
compulsory levies. OBN begins from a different premise — that directing value
toward public benefit should never require an owner to give up what they own.

Tracing that premise downward, by repeatedly asking "why should this be true,"
produces a short chain:

1. **Generosity shouldn't require sacrifice.** If doing good always costs the
   doer the thing they'd have to give up, less good gets done, because the
   threshold for participating becomes "I have enough surplus to afford the
   loss" — a bar most people never clear.
2. **Ownership is not absolute private dominion.** What an asset produces is
   never created by its owner in total isolation — its value depends on a
   shared context (markets, trust, the existence of other people) the owner
   didn't create alone.
3. **Reciprocity is a condition of legitimate participation in any
   interdependent system.** Drawing benefit from shared cooperation carries an
   obligation to contribute back to sustaining it.

Going one step further than (3) requires leaving political philosophy
entirely — into either a metaphysical claim about the nature of persons, or a
formal meta-ethical argument for why reciprocity itself binds. Both are real
questions; neither sharpens anything about how to build OBN. That's where the
regress was deliberately stopped.

## Why the Charter doesn't say any of this

The reasoning above is a *design rationale* — it explains why an architect
might choose to build this system. A founding charter has a different job: to
state what the institution does and is bound to do, in terms acceptable to
people who hold entirely different reasons for valuing it.

This is close to what political philosophy calls an *overlapping consensus*: a
stable public institution doesn't require everyone to share one comprehensive
worldview. It requires people holding different worldviews to be able to
endorse the same operating rules, each for their own reasons — duty, faith,
self-interest, reputation, or simple preference.

Concretely, this meant rewriting what was originally drafted as a normative
claim —

> "Ownership is stewardship, not dominion."

— into a purely descriptive one:

> "Olive Branch Network exists to make it possible for a person to preserve
> ownership of their assets while continuously directing a portion of the
> value those assets generate toward independently accountable public
> benefit."

Someone who believes in absolute, unqualified property rights can use OBN
without endorsing the stewardship argument above. The mechanism doesn't
require agreement on the philosophy — only acceptance of the rules. That's
deliberate, and it's why the Charter (see `../charter/CHARTER.md`) is written
the way it is.

## Lineage

OBN's underlying premise — that ownership can be separated from the benefit it
generates, without surrendering the asset — is not new. It echoes:

- **Locke's proviso** that appropriation is only just if it leaves "enough and
  as good" for others.
- **Stewardship doctrines** found across many religious and ethical
  traditions, treating wealth as held in trust rather than owned outright.
- **Georgist economics**, which argues unearned value from shared social
  conditions belongs partly to the commons.
- **Modern steward-ownership and stakeholder-capital movements**, which argue
  capital carries obligations beyond its immediate holder.

What's new is not the idea — it's that programmable assets let the idea be
enforced automatically and continuously, rather than depending on each
owner's individual memory or virtue.

It's also worth being precise about what OBN's axioms are *not* doing. Bitcoin
and Ethereum's foundational beliefs — rules over rulers, agreements
enforceable without centralized trust — are about the legitimacy of *process*.
OBN's axioms (see `../charter/CHARTER.md`, Articles II–VI) are mostly about
the legitimacy of a *claim*: what someone rightfully and exclusively owns,
versus what's owed back to the system that made it valuable. The exception is
the Commons axiom (Article V), which is the one place OBN's reasoning is
closer to crypto-native "rules over rulers" thinking than to property-ethics
tradition — a deliberate choice about where authority over unclaimed value
should sit, not a restatement of Article I.

## The standard this is held to

A framework like this earns trust by surviving contact with real engineering
decisions, not by being elaborated further in the abstract. See `../cdr/` for
the record of whether it has.
