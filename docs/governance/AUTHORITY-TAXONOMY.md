# OBN Authority Taxonomy

A vocabulary for classifying decisions — not a new layer of philosophy or
engineering. Its only job is to make "what kind of decision is this"
answerable consistently, so whether something needs a vote doesn't get
re-litigated from scratch every time it comes up.

| Authority Type | Examples | Who decides | Charter basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Constitutional** | Amending the Charter itself | Governance, via Article X's process | Article X |
| **Governance** | Voting on disposition of undesignated value | Participants, collectively | Article V |
| **Policy** | Which categories of organization currently qualify as Public Benefit | Today: protocol operators, under Article IX | Article IX |
| **Operational** | Data ingestion, verification refreshes, routine recipient re-checks | Whoever runs the relevant process | Article IV (execution of, not interpretation of) |
| **Engineering** | Refactors, consolidations, or implementation changes that don't change constitutional meaning | Ordinary engineering review | Article VIII (must still pass the Constraint) |

## A caution about this table

This table describes today's classification — it is not a settled answer to
every question it touches. CDR #1 explicitly left open whether recipient
*eligibility*, classified above as Policy, should instead be Governance.
Introducing this taxonomy is not a quiet way of resolving that question by
definitional fiat: the table reflects current Article IX practice, not a
ruling on whether that practice is correct.

If a future CDR proposes moving a decision from one row to another — Policy
to Governance, say — that reclassification is itself a decision this
taxonomy should be used to classify in turn. Changing who holds authority
over something is plausibly Constitutional, not Policy, even when the
underlying decision (which nonprofits qualify) looks like an ordinary Policy
matter on its surface.

## How to use it

Every CDR states which Authority Type the decision it records belongs to. If
a decision doesn't cleanly fit one row, that mismatch is itself worth
recording as an open question — the same way CDR #1 did.
